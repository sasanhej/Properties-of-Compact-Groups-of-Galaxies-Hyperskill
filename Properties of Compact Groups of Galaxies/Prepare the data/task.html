Description

In this project, we will explore the properties of compact groups of galaxies (CGs) in order to develop data analysis skills. We will work with a dataset designed for and by astrophysicists studying CGs.

The dataset columns contain the following information:

  • Group — the group name;
  • z — redshift, a value that indicates the distance to the object;
  • mean_mu — the surface brightness of stars that are located between galaxies, called intra-group light (IGL);
  • features — the presence of visible signatures around the galaxies of CG, called low surface brightness (LSB) feature: 1 if yes, 0 if no.

Theory

Let's learn more about essential terms.

  • The apparent magnitude \(m\) tells how bright a star or any other source appears to us at a distance from the Earth. The scale is logarithmic and inverse. A larger magnitude corresponds to a fainter source. In our case, the surface brightness is quoted on a magnitude scale — in magnitudes per square arcsecond, usually denoted by the Greek letter \(\mu\). For more information, refer to the article Apparent versus Absolute Magnitude by Learn the Sky.
  • A redshift indicates the distance to the object. It is an increase in the wavelength of electromagnetic radiation caused solely by the expansion of the Universe. As the Universe expands, it stretches light that travels through space in a cosmological redshift phenomenon. The greater the redshift, the greater the distance the light has traveled. The Cosmological Redshift (YouTube) video by Jason Kendall can shed more light on this.
  • An intra-group light (IGL) is the faint light from stars between galaxy groups. The vast majority of stars in galaxy groups are within their constituent galaxies. However, a small fraction of stars are expected to follow the global dark matter potential of the group. These stars form an IGL component.
  • Within the Lambda cold dark matter (ΛCDM) cosmological model, galaxy interactions play a major role in the hierarchical galaxy formation theory. The hierarchical structure formation paradigm requires every galaxy to undergo interactions throughout its lifetime. Each interaction leaves visible signatures around galaxies in faint shells, tidal tails, halos, plumes, and stellar streams. These low surface brightness (LSB) features around galaxies encode the hierarchical nature of the cosmological structure formation. Modern LSB methods of observation can detect these features. To get a complete picture of this field of astronomy, you can study this review article: The Vera Rubin Observatory Legacy Survey of Space and Time and the Low Surface Brightness Universe. Objectives

Examine the box plot below for the mean surface brightness of the IGL in a group of compact groups of galaxies (CGs) with low surface brightness (LSB) features and without them. Try to plot the same chart. Then find a mean redshift for these CGs.

The surface brightness is quoted on an inverse magnitude scale, so to place the brighter sources at the top, astronomers usually invert the magnitude axis.

Try to answer the questions:

  • Is the surface brightness of the IGL in a group higher for groups of galaxies with or without LSB features?
  • Are groups of galaxies with LSB features further or closer to us?

To complete this stage:

  1. Read the following dataset: groups.tsv. TSV stands for tab-separated values. Use the pandas.read_csv method with the parameter delimiter='\t';
  2. Remove the missing values from the dataset. You can use the dropna function;
  3. Find a mean value for the IGL mean surface brightness in groups of galaxies with LSB features and without them;
  4. Print two floating-point numbers separated by a space: the mean value of the mean surface brightness for galaxies with and without LSB features.
